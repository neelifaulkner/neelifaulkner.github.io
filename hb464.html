HB 464.

The DHR Central Registry and Due Process Act. This bill is about registry accuracy, staff training, and basic due process.

Scroll for the plain breakdown.

scroll

The problem

  • People are being placed on the statewide registry without first receiving a formal “indicated” finding. Due process concerns.
  • Registry placement can cause reputational harm and social stigma, even when allegations are unsubstantiated.
  • It can lead to job loss, professional disqualification, and licensing barriers, especially for positions involving children or vulnerable people.
  • It may also impact custody determinations and family court proceedings.
  • Alabama law does not require a training program before DHR employees conduct investigations.
  • Alabama law does not clearly require an annual legislative report on investigation outcomes and child placement data.
Bottom line: People should not face life changing consequences without clear standards and a fair process.

What HB 464 does

  • Prohibits DHR from putting not indicated reports, or related information, on the registry. Requires removal of not indicated reports already on the registry. Retroactive.
  • Prohibits an indicated report from being placed on the registry unless confirmed by an investigative hearing, or the subject waives the hearing.
  • Requires DHR to notify the subject they are under investigation, with limited safety exemptions.
  • After a preliminary indicated finding, requires notice and the opportunity for an investigative hearing.
  • Requires a training program for DHR employees involved in investigations, and requires employees to undergo that training.
  • Requires an annual report to key House and Senate committees on investigation outcomes and child placement data.
  • Establishes due process rights at the hearing. Counsel, evidence review, witnesses, cross-examination, subpoenas, and an impartial hearing officer.
  • Revises definitions of abuse and neglect and revises standards for indicated findings.
It protects children while strengthening due process, registry accuracy, staff training, and accountability within DHR.

Child removal standards

  • Strengthens the standard for removal by requiring credible evidence.
  • Requires investigators to consider family-preservation factors before removal.

Criminal warrant limits

  • Limits use of DHR indicated reports for criminal warrants and requires law enforcement corroboration.
  • Restricts service of warrants relying on DHR records unless a grand jury indictment has been returned.
